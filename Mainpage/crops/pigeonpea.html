Procedures to Grow Pigeon Peas

Pigeon Pea Details

Name: Cajanus cajan
Origin: Pigeon peas, also known as tur dal or arhar dal, originated in India and have been cultivated for thousands of years.
Characteristics: Pigeon pea plants are perennial legumes with woody stems and compound leaves. They produce yellow flowers that develop into pods containing 2-9 seeds each. The seeds, which are round and vary in color from yellow to brown, are the edible part of the plant.

Pigeon Pea Growing Procedure

January to March (Preparation and Planning)

Site Selection and Land Preparation:
Choose a sunny location with well-drained soil for planting pigeon peas.
Clear the field of weeds, debris, and any obstacles that may hinder planting or growth.
Conduct soil tests to determine nutrient levels and pH, and amend the soil as needed.

Soil Preparation:
Till the soil to a depth of 6-8 inches to loosen it and improve aeration.
Incorporate organic matter, such as compost or aged manure, to enrich the soil and provide essential nutrients.
Create raised beds or rows for planting, with spacing appropriate for the pigeon pea variety.

Seed Selection and Treatment:
Choose high-quality pigeon pea seeds suited to your climate and soil conditions.
Treat seeds with fungicides or insecticides to protect against seed-borne diseases and pests.

April to June (Planting and Early Growth)

Planting:
Sow pigeon pea seeds directly into the prepared soil once the danger of frost has passed and soil temperatures reach at least 70°F (21°C).
Plant seeds at a depth of 1-2 inches and spacing of 12-24 inches apart within rows, with rows spaced 24-36 inches apart.
Ensure adequate soil moisture for germination and early growth by watering gently after planting.

Water Management:
Maintain consistent soil moisture throughout the growing season, especially during periods of drought or heat stress.
Water pigeon pea plants deeply and evenly, providing 1-1.5 inches of water per week, either through rainfall or irrigation.

July to September (Growth and Development)

Nutrient Management:
Apply balanced fertilizers, rich in nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium, to support pigeon pea growth and development.
Side-dress nitrogen fertilizer when plants begin to flower to encourage vigorous growth and pod development.

Weed Control:
Keep the pigeon pea field free from weeds, which compete with plants for nutrients, water, and sunlight.
Use mechanical cultivation, mulching, or herbicides to control weed growth as needed, taking care to avoid damage to pigeon pea plants.

October to December (Maturation and Harvesting)

Ripening Stage:
Monitor pigeon pea plants for signs of maturity, including yellowing and drying of leaves and pods.
Allow pigeon pea pods to mature fully on the plant, as indicated by a firm texture and a change in color from green to tan or brown.

Harvesting:
Harvest pigeon peas when pods are fully mature and dry, typically 150-180 days after planting, depending on the variety and local conditions.
Test a few pods for readiness by pressing them between your fingers; if the seeds are hard and dry, the pigeon peas are ready for harvest.
Use sharp knives or shears to cut pigeon pea plants at ground level, then collect the pods and remove the seeds.

Post-Harvest Management:
Shell pigeon pea pods to release the seeds, then clean and dry the seeds thoroughly before storage.
Store dried pigeon peas in a cool, dry place in airtight containers to maintain quality and prevent insect infestations.
